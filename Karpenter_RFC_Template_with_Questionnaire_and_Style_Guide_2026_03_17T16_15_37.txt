Karpenter RFC Template î Questionnaire + LLM Style GuideHow to Use This Template1. Answer the questionnaire under each section heading. Your answers can be rough notes, bullet points, or full sentences î the LLM will shape them.2. Hand the completed questionnaire to an LLM along with the Section Style Guides and the Master Style Guide at the end.3. The LLM will produce a polished RFC document ready for review.SECTION 1: Title & MetadataQuestionnaire? RFC Title: What is a short, descriptive title for this proposal? (e.g., "Consolidation Cost Threshold")? Authors: Who is authoring this RFC? (names and aliases)? Status: What is the current status? (Draft / In Review / Accepted / Abandoned / Superseded)? Created Date: When was this RFC first drafted?? Last Updated: When was this RFC last modified?? Reviewers: Who are the expected reviewers or stakeholders?? Tracking Issue/PR: Link to the GitHub issue or PR tracking this RFC (if any).Section Style Guide for LLM? Present metadata in a clean key-value table at the top of the document.? Title should be concise (ý10 words), descriptive, and avoid jargon where possible.? Status should use one of the canonical values listed above.SECTION 2: SummaryQuestionnaire? One-liner: In one sentence, what does this RFC propose?? What changes: What will be different after this RFC is implemented? (new behavior, new API field, new algorithm, etc.)? Who benefits: Who is the primary audience or beneficiary? (end users, operators, contributors, etc.)Section Style Guide for LLM? Write exactly 2Å4 sentences. No more.? First sentence states what the RFC proposes. Second sentence states why it matters. Optional third/fourth sentences briefly describe the scope or approach.? Avoid implementation details î this is an executive summary.? Use active voice and present tense ("This RFC proposes..." not "It is proposed that...").SECTION 3: MotivationQuestionnaire? Problem statement: What problem exists today? Describe the pain point or gap.? Who is affected: Which users, personas, or systems experience this problem?? Impact: What happens if we do nothing? What's the cost of inaction?? User stories (optional): Provide 1Å3 "As a [role], I want [goal], so that [benefit]" statements.? Evidence: Any data, customer feedback, issues, or incidents that illustrate the problem?Section Style Guide for LLM? Lead with the problem, not the solution.? Use concrete examples or scenarios to make the pain point tangible.? If user stories are provided, format them as a bulleted list with bold role labels.? Keep this section focused on why, not how. Do not preview the solution here.? 1Å3 paragraphs plus optional user stories.SECTION 4: Goals and Non-GoalsQuestionnaire? Goals: What specific outcomes must this RFC achieve? List 3Å6 goals.? Non-Goals: What is explicitly out of scope? What might someone assume is included but isn't? List 2Å4 non-goals.? Future Goals (optional): What might be addressed in a follow-up but is deferred for now?Section Style Guide for LLM? Present Goals and Non-Goals as separate bulleted lists.? Each item should be a single, clear declarative statement.? Goals should be testable/verifiable î avoid vague aspirations like "improve performance" without qualification.? Non-Goals should preempt scope creep by naming specific things the RFC will not do.? Future Goals (if present) go in a third list, clearly labeled as deferred.SECTION 5: Proposal / DesignQuestionnaire? High-level approach: In 2Å3 sentences, what is the proposed solution at a high level?? Detailed design: Walk through the design. Consider:? What new components, fields, or behaviors are introduced?? How does data flow through the system?? What are the key algorithms or decision points?? What API changes are needed (new fields, new CRDs, modified status conditions)?? Configuration: What new configuration options are exposed to users? What are the defaults?? Diagrams (optional): Describe any architecture or sequence diagrams you'd like included (the LLM can generate ASCII or Mermaid diagrams).? Examples: Provide 1Å2 concrete examples showing the feature in action (e.g., sample YAML, before/after behavior).Section Style Guide for LLM? Start with a 2Å3 sentence overview before diving into details.? Use subheadings (###) to break the design into logical components.? Use code blocks for YAML examples, API snippets, or pseudocode.? When describing algorithms, decompose into numbered steps with clear inputs, outputs, and invariants.? Frame complex problems through multiple conceptual lenses where appropriate (e.g., end-member analysis, configuration-driven behavior, temporal optimization).? Organize system properties into categories (Safety, Efficiency, Adaptability, Modularity) when relevant.? Keep paragraphs short (3Å5 sentences). Use bullet lists for enumerations.? Prefer concrete examples over abstract descriptions.SECTION 6: Alternatives ConsideredQuestionnaire? Alternative 1: Describe another approach you considered. Why was it rejected?? Alternative 2: Describe another approach. Why was it rejected?? Alternative 3 (optional): Any additional alternatives?? Do nothing: What happens if we don't implement this RFC at all?Section Style Guide for LLM? Present each alternative as a subsection (### Alternative: [Name]).? For each alternative, include: a brief description (1Å2 sentences), pros, cons, and reason for rejection.? Use a consistent structure across all alternatives for easy comparison.? The "Do Nothing" alternative should always be included as the baseline.? Be fair to rejected alternatives î acknowledge their strengths before explaining why the proposed approach is better.SECTION 7: Risks and MitigationsQuestionnaire? Risk 1: What could go wrong? What's the mitigation?? Risk 2: What's another risk? Mitigation?? Risk 3 (optional): Additional risks?? Backward compatibility: Does this change break any existing behavior? How is backward compatibility maintained?? Security implications: Are there any security considerations?? Performance implications: Could this change degrade performance? Under what conditions?Section Style Guide for LLM? Present as a table with columns: Risk, Severity (Low/Medium/High), Mitigation.? After the table, expand on any High-severity risks with a short paragraph.? Backward compatibility, security, and performance should each get their own subsection if the author provided substantive answers.? Be direct about risks î don't minimize them. Credibility comes from honest assessment.SECTION 8: Testing and ValidationQuestionnaire? Unit tests: What unit tests will validate this change?? Integration tests: What integration or end-to-end tests are needed?? Manual validation: How can a reviewer or user manually verify the behavior?? Edge cases: What edge cases should be tested?? Metrics/observability: What metrics, logs, or events will be added to monitor this feature?Section Style Guide for LLM? Use a bulleted list for each test category.? Be specific î name the scenarios, not just "unit tests will be added."? Edge cases should be called out explicitly with expected behavior.? Metrics/observability should specify metric names, labels, and what they measure.SECTION 9: Migration and Rollout PlanQuestionnaire? Feature gating: Will this be behind a feature gate or flag? What's the default?? Rollout phases: Is there a phased rollout? (e.g., opt-in ? default-on ? mandatory)? Migration steps: Do existing users need to take any action? What's the upgrade path?? Rollback plan: How do we revert if something goes wrong?? Documentation: What documentation needs to be created or updated?Section Style Guide for LLM? Present rollout phases as a numbered list or timeline.? Migration steps should be written as clear, actionable instructions.? Rollback plan should be explicit and concrete î not "we can revert."? Keep this section practical and operator-focused.SECTION 10: Open QuestionsQuestionnaire? Question 1: What's an unresolved question you want feedback on?? Question 2: Another open question?? Question 3 (optional): Additional questions?? Decision needed from reviewers: Are there specific decisions you need reviewers to weigh in on?Section Style Guide for LLM? Present as a numbered list.? Each question should be self-contained and clearly stated.? If there are proposed answers or leanings, include them in parentheses after the question.? Tag questions with the stakeholder group best suited to answer (e.g., "[For SIG-Autoscaling]").MASTER STYLE GUIDE î For the LLM Writing the Final RFCVoice and Tone? Write in first-person plural ("we propose", "our approach") to reflect team authorship.? Active voice throughout. Avoid passive constructions.? Confident but not arrogant î state positions clearly, acknowledge trade-offs honestly.? Plain language î prefer simple, direct sentences. Avoid jargon unless it's standard Kubernetes/Karpenter terminology.? Technical precision î use correct terminology for Kubernetes concepts (Pods, Nodes, NodeClaims, NodePools, etc.). Do not simplify at the cost of accuracy.Structure and Formatting? Use Markdown formatting throughout.? Section headers use ##. Subsections use ###. Do not go deeper than ####.? Keep paragraphs to 3Å5 sentences. Break up walls of text.? Use bulleted lists for enumerations, options, and properties.? Use numbered lists only for sequential steps or ranked items.? Use tables for comparisons, risk matrices, and multi-attribute data.? Use code blocks (triple backticks) for YAML, Go, pseudocode, or CLI examples.? Use bold for key terms on first introduction and for emphasis in lists.? Use italics sparingly for mild emphasis or to reference document titles.Content Principles? Motivation before solution î always establish the "why" before the "what."? Concrete over abstract î prefer examples, scenarios, and sample configs over theoretical descriptions.? Decompose complexity î break complex designs into distinct stages with clear inputs, outputs, and invariants.? Multiple lenses î when analyzing a complex problem, consider framing it through different conceptual approaches (end-member/extremes analysis, configuration-driven behavior, algorithmic decision sequences, temporal optimization, formal methods) where it adds clarity.? Organize properties î when describing system behavior, categorize properties (Safety, Efficiency, Adaptability, Modularity) to help readers reason about trade-offs.? Honest trade-offs î every design decision has costs. Name them. This builds credibility and invites constructive feedback.? Feedback-oriented î the RFC exists to gather feedback and improve the design. Frame open questions as invitations, not admissions of weakness.Length and Scope? Target 1,500Å3,000 words for the full RFC (excluding code examples and tables).? The Summary should be the shortest section (2Å4 sentences).? The Proposal/Design section will typically be the longest (40Å50% of the document).? If a section has no content from the questionnaire, omit it from the final document rather than including an empty section.Karpenter-Specific Conventions? Reference Karpenter concepts correctly: NodePool, NodeClaim, EC2NodeClass (for AWS), Disruption Budgets, Consolidation, etc.? When referencing Kubernetes upstream concepts, use standard terminology (Pod, Node, Deployment, etc.).? If the design touches scope owned by a Kubernetes SIG, note this explicitly and recommend socialization with that SIG.? Link to relevant existing RFCs, design docs, or GitHub issues where appropriate.Final Checklist for the LLMBefore outputting the final RFC, verify:? ( At least one concrete example is in the Proposal section? ( "Do Nothing" is listed as an alternative? ( Risks table includes severity ratings?
